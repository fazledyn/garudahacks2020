Mathematics for Industrial Level

The reason why economists adopted mathematics as the language (and yes it is a language!) to create their models is that it is super super hard to be imprecise with mathematics. Sure, it can be hard for a layman or even fellow economists to undersrand, but when you use mathematics as a language to model economic phenomena, it forces you to be very explicit about your (often unrealistic) assumptions and how you prove your results. It's really easy to claim in blocks of text that certain things cause certain economic phenomena and obfuscate it with the English language to hide any logical fallacies/inconsistencies. However, it is really difficult to do this with mathematics, so when writing a proof of something, it's really easy to pick up where a person's model is actually false.

The second big reason is that we want to be able to show quantitatively causal links and forecast the effects of policies and other economic phenomena. Using the tools of mathematics, we can take economic intuition and insight and rigorously develop a model that both establishes under what conditions we might observe x causing y but also to what degree x can affect y. Calculus and dynamic programmingfor example enable economists to show how agents optimize their decisions over a time horizon and with uncertainty. Economists often also use what are called fixed point theorems to prove the existence of equilibrium in an economy. Without mathematics, all we could do is claim x causes y and not be able to show how it occurs except through vague wording and will still be unable to provide precise predictions as to how it works. For example, it's really easy to say supply and demand equate in general equilibrium because shortages and surpluses will drive prices up and down but it's much harder to show that given a set of n goods with time and uncertainty, can economic forces influence prices such that they clear all markets by equating supply and demand?

I also wanted to add one thing about Ryan's comment about big data modeling. Although big data is certainly affecting the economics profession and will be a useful tool, a huge gaping issue with big data is that it's primarily a predictive statistical tool. Data scientists are more interested in finding what relationships exist in data than finding out why they occur. Big data thus is really good at predicting behavior but it fails to explain what causes people to do certain things and is therefore not the greatest tool to determine how a policy change might affect the economy (like a tax cut). Economists, however, don't want to just find statistical associations. They want tk establish causal relationships and figure out what causes what in the economy.
